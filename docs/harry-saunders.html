Who We Are

Harry Saunders

Senior Fellow, Conceptual Investigations Unit

Harry Saunders is a Senior Fellow at the Conceptual Investigations Unit. He is also Managing Director of Decision Processes Incorporated.

Background

Harry Saunders is a Senior Fellow at the Conceptual Investigations Unit and Managing Director of Decision Processes Incorporated, where he advises corporations on strategic decision-making under uncertainty. An economist of energy and energy efficiency, he is widely regarded as a founding figure in the study of the energy-efficiency rebound effect — he coined the term "Khazzoom–Brookes Postulate," the idea that gains in energy efficiency can lead, paradoxically, to greater overall energy use. His earlier career included serving as Manager of Strategy at the Tosco Corporation and work with the U.S. Department of Commerce and the International Energy Agency. Beyond energy, his interests range across evolutionary biology and the theory of evidence in law.

Selected scientific contributions

Energy efficiency and the rebound effect

  • Empirical evidence that rebound effects are large enough to materially change the conclusions of climate-change models (Saunders, 2015).
  • A documentation of energy-efficiency rebound across thirty U.S. economic sectors, with a toolkit for other rebound analysts (Saunders, 2013).
  • A production-function framework clarifying when efficiency improvements conserve energy and when they increase its use (Saunders, 2008).
  • A macroeconomic account of rebound, backfire, and the Khazzoom–Brookes postulate (Saunders, 2000), together with an analysis of how rebound estimates depend on distinguishing energy from the services it provides (Saunders, 2000).
  • The paper that named the Khazzoom–Brookes postulate and set it within neoclassical growth theory (Saunders, 1992).

Energy efficiency, policy, and security

  • A multi-author review assessing what four decades of energy-efficiency research has delivered (Saunders et al., 2021).
  • An exergy-based, national-level method finding rebound large enough to threaten low-carbon goals (Brockway et al., 2017).
  • An examination of how bundled policies interact in the pursuit of energy security (Sovacool and Saunders, 2014).
  • An energy-economics assessment of whether solid-state (LED) lighting will reduce or expand overall energy use (Tsao et al., 2010).
